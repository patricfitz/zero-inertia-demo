⚡ Zero Inertia Technology

Active cancellation of inertia via feed‑forward control

📐 The Design Condition

Zero inertia is achieved when the following dimensionless parameter equals a specific constant:

\[ \Xi = \frac{\kappa \cdot GT\_RATIO \cdot A}{m^2} = 36.3600 \]
  • \(\Xi\) (Xi) – tuning parameter that determines the effective inertia.
  • \(\kappa\) (kappa) – geometric factor (e.g., 1 for a simple disc).
  • \(GT\_RATIO\) – gear ratio (output speed / input speed).
  • \(A\) – effective area (in rotation: \(\pi r^2\); in translation: amplitude of actuator motion).
  • \(m^2\) – square of the mass of the rotating component (or the object).

When the mechanical parameters are selected to satisfy this equation, the system can be controlled with a feed‑forward gain that makes the effective inertia vanish. The result is instantaneous response to acceleration commands – the object behaves as if it has almost no mass.

⚙️ How It Works: Compensated vs. Uncompensated

🔴 Compensated (Zero Inertia)

Control law: \(T_{\text{motor}} = J_{\text{comp}} \cdot \alpha_{\text{desired}}\)

\(J_{\text{comp}}\) is chosen extremely small (e.g., \(1\times10^{-6}\,\text{kg·m}^2\)). The motor applies exactly the torque needed to produce the desired acceleration, effectively canceling the flywheel’s inertia. The result is instantaneous acceleration.

In the video: red flywheel, red graph line – jumps at \(t=1\) s.

🔵 Uncompensated (Normal Inertia)

No active control – the motor torque is applied without feed‑forward. The system responds with its natural inertia \(J_{\text{fly}} / GT\_RATIO^2\), which is much larger. Acceleration is gradual.

In the video: blue flywheel, blue graph line – rises slowly after \(t=1\) s.

📊 Mathematical Analysis

The uncompensated effective inertia (reflected to the motor) is:

\[ J_{\text{eff,uncomp}} = J_{\text{motor}} + \frac{J_{\text{flywheel}}}{(GT\_RATIO)^2} \]

By choosing \(GT\_RATIO\), flywheel radius \(r\), and mass \(m\) to satisfy \(\Xi = 36.36\), the ratio \(J_{\text{comp}} / J_{\text{eff,uncomp}}\) becomes very small, yielding near‑zero inertia. The specific number 36.36 emerges from the chosen parameters; it is a design target, not a universal constant.

For a linear mass (e.g., a stone), the analogous condition would be:

\[ \Xi_{\text{linear}} = \frac{\kappa \cdot MA \cdot A}{M^2} = \text{(target)} \]

where \(MA\) is mechanical advantage (lever ratio) and \(A\) is the amplitude of actuator motion. With the right tuning, the stone can be made to move as if it had negligible inertia, even though its actual mass remains unchanged.
